01

Core architecture

The bus is a shared, dual-redundant twisted-shielded-pair cable. One Bus Controller (BC) owns all traffic initiation; up to 31 Remote Terminals (RT) only speak when addressed; an optional Bus Monitor (BM) listens passively for diagnostics.

FIG. 1 — Dual-redundant bus topology with stub-coupled terminals
Bus controller

One active BC at a time. Owns the schedule; nothing transmits without a command.

Remote terminal

5-bit address (0–30). Address 31 reserved for broadcast — 31 usable RTs.

Bus monitor

Passive listener for logging/diagnostics. Never transmits into the exchange.

02

Word format

Every word on the bus is 20 bits, Manchester II biphase encoded at 1 Mbps — self-clocking and DC-balanced, which suits transformer coupling.

04

Status word & service request

The status word's bit 8 is the Service Request flag (SRQ). It's how an RT signals it has something urgent, without ever interrupting the bus.

FIG. 4 — Status word bit layout, service request highlighted
Important nuance

SRQ does not interrupt the bus. The RT cannot transmit on its own — it only piggybacks the flag on its next scheduled status word. The BC decides if and when to act, typically by issuing a Transmit Vector Word mode code. This keeps the core rule intact: the BC is always in control, RTs only ever respond.

05

Stubbing

Stubbing is how a terminal physically taps into the main bus without disrupting its electrical characteristics. Two coupling methods are defined.

FIG. 5 — Transformer-coupled (isolated) vs. direct-coupled (short) stub
MethodMax lengthIsolationTrade-off
Transformer-coupled20 ft (6 m)Full galvanic isolationBulkier, needs coupler box
Direct-coupled1 ft (30 cm)Isolation resistors onlyTerminal fault can affect main bus
Why it matters

An overly long or improperly terminated stub causes signal reflections that corrupt the Manchester-encoded bits. Strict length limits keep impedance mismatch small enough not to distort the waveform for other terminals sharing the bus.
